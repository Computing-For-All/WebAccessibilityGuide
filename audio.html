    Audio

    With over 450 million people in the world with hearing disability, having your website accessible to those with hearing disability is essential. Designing an experience accessible to the hearing impared is challenging and given that audio elements are usually paired together with visual elements, knowing how to be accessible to the visually impared is also likely required. This article will walk through the basics of audio accessibility with respect to audio only experiences.

    Who

    Those most benefiting to an audio accessible website experience includes but not limited to:

    • Those who were born or lived most of their lives with hearing loss (Deaf)
    • People who lost hearing ability later in life (deaf)
    • Individuals who have recently lost some or all ability to sense audio either temporarily or permanently



    Overview

    Examples of elements that enhance audio accessibility are:

    • Transcripts
    • Captions
    • Sign Language



    Transcripts

    Transcripts can be basic transcripts or descriptive transcripts. Basic transcripts can be used for media that doesnâ€™t require the user to comprehend the visual elements to understand the content of said media, while descriptive transcripts should be used otherwise. Interactive transcripts are usually good for accessibility and are common among media players. The process of transcribing is not an easy skill and it is paramount that visual information is included in the transcripts if applicable. How transcripts are added to the website experiences varies, but inserted as html is common.




    Captions

    Captions are different from transcripts because captions are visual representations of the media relayed to the user in real time in relation to the media while transcripts are comprehensive visual representations of the media that is not real time. Typically creating captions is a demanding task and usually handled by third-party professional caption companies. Auto generated captions are almost always not sufficient to be accessible because even a single misplaced word (like the word not) can significantly misrepresent the content of the media. WebVTT is a common caption format but there are also others out there.




    Sign Langauage

    For many hearing impared individuals (especially the Deaf), sign language is how they communicate. Sign language is a way for the audio aspect of a media to be portrayed visually and there are many types of sign languages out there. Typically someone communicating in sign language is either played in a popup that runs in parallel with the video, or is part of the video itself. Sign Language may be the most steep avenue of providing audio accessibility to your website because it often requires having someone who knows sign languages to translate your experience.




    Media Player

    Making your own media playing and incorporating it into your website is often challenging thus an excellent option is using a third-party web-based media player. Some populars ones are Able Player and video.js.




    Vocab

    Basic Transcripts: Transcripts contains text representation of audio but without text representation of visual information like setting

    Descriptive Transcripts: Transcripts contains text representation of audio and text representation of visual information like setting

    Interactive Transcripts: Transcript that highlights text while audio is playing, also text acts as timestamps on the video which the user can use to move thoughout the video.

    Transcribing: Skill and Act of translating a form of media to a text representation.

    Paramount: Important.

    Auto Generated: Content generated by instructions rather than manually from humans.

    Avenue: A means to a end; a path

    Third-Party: Typically in a interaction between a client and a consumer, the third party is a group which provides services for the interaction but isn't affilated with either the client or consumer.
