    Visual Accessibility

    The different visual abilities and the diverse nature of impairments create a wide range of variations that a website design may be perceived. As such, it is important and beneficial for all users to have designs that follow standardized criteria and are easy to navigate. A clean and clear visual representation would allow visitors to feel at ease when exploring the website's information and functionality.







    Color & Contrast

    For individuals with color blindness and low vision, getting enough contrast between the text and background is essential.

    Steps to Take

    • Contrast ratio between the text and background is recommended to be larger than 4.5:1 for small text and 3:1 for large text.
      • Accessible color palettes and combinations: Accessible Color Palette Builder.
      • A tool to measure contrasts between text and background: WebAIM’s Color Contrast Checker.
    • Aim for a more temper contrast between your text and background: avoid using pure black with pure white for contrast.
    • If there is text over an image, use a dark overlay over the image or add a solid background behind the text.
    • A study has shown that warmer background shades improve text-readability with dyslexia.
      • The research paper: Good Background Colors for Readers: A Study of People with and without Dyslexia
    • Other than just color, use icons, words, and other visual elements to reinforce clear communication of the content

    Layout

    Having an information-overloaded website may be as undesirable as having no information on a page at all. For people with visual impairments and who are easily distracted, navigating through an information-dense website is even more challenging. Therefore, it is important to ensure the accessibility of a website by designing the layout to be clear and concise.

    Steps to Take

    • Make key information discernable at a glance through clear headings, highlight of keywords, and so on.
    • Design minimally, intentionally, and predictably so that users can easily and quickly find important information on the page.
    • Place items on the screen according to their relative level of importance so that important actions are either at the top, bottom, or on a side navigation bar. This way, important items are more accessible.
    • Give important actions and links clickable cues.
    • Plan heading structure early and ensure that all content and design fits into a logical heading structure.
    • Make sure reading order is consistent with visual order. Most common reading order is left-to-right.
    • Group related items in proximity to one another to help those who have low vision or trouble focusing on the screen.

    Vocab

    Clickable Cues: a visual indication that a given word or item on a Web page is clickable. Cues that can be used to indicate the clickability of an item include color, underlining, bullets, and arrows.

    Color Blindness: partial or total inability to distinguish one or more colors.

    Dyslexia: a general term for disorders that involve difficulty in learning to read or interpret words, letters, and other symbols, but that do not affect general intelligence.

    Text-Readability: a measure of how easy a piece of text is to read.
